    Section 1.1 Solving Quadratic Equations the 20th Century Way

    Do I really need to review quadratic equations? If you are confident with your algebra and calculus skills, you may not need a refresher in quadratic equations.
    🔗
    Here is a good litmus test. Can you solve the following by hand?
    \begin{equation*} 5e^{x-1} - \frac{7}{3}e^{2x-2}-\frac{4}{3}=0 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    If so, then you many be in a good place to move to the next section. If not, I recommend reviewing this section.
    🔗
    In an age with myriad phone apps that can solve transcendental equations to high degrees of accuracy in nanoseconds, P Exam candidates need to solve some equations by hand (with a little help from your TI-30XS MultiView). We’ll assume that a review of solving linear equations is not necessary and move right into solving a quadratic equation.
    🔗

    Definition 1.1.1.

    A quadratic equation in variable \(x\) is an equation that can be written in the form \(ax^2 + bx + c = 0\) where \(a, b,\) and \(c\) are real numbers.
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 1.1.1 Graphs of Quadratic Equations

    A quadratic equation, like any real-valued function, can be graphed on the Cartesian plane. It turns out that figures called parabolas, which are geometric curves defined by things called directrices and foci (don’t worry about it), are modeled by quadratic equations. Recall from your algebra studies that a parabola is that U-shaped curve that can open upward or downward. Here are some perfectly good parabolas below.
    🔗
    Algebraically, whether a parabola opens upward or downward depends on the coefficient of the \(x^2\) term in the parabola’s equation. In the equation \(y=ax^2+bx+c\text{,}\) the parabola will open upward if \(a>0\) and downward if \(a<0\text{.}\) (If \(a=0\text{,}\) the we have a line rather than a parabola.)
    🔗
    For many parabolas, some points of interest include the points where the curve intersects the \(x\)-axis. For a quadratic equation \(ax^2 + bx+c\) representing such a parabola, the \(x\)-values of those points would be the solutions to \(ax^2 + bx + c = 0\text{.}\) The solutions to such an equation are called the \textit{roots} of the equation. As can be seen in the above figure, not all quadratic equations have roots as not all parabolas intersect the $x$-axis. Further, some quadratic equations only have one root.
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 1.1.2 Finding the Roots of a Quadratic Equation

    As you may recall from your algebra studies, there are a few ways to find the roots of a quadratic equation by hand. We will only review the universal method of finding roots: the quadratic formula.Quadratic formula}
    🔗

    Definition 1.1.3.

    The solutions to a quadratic equation of the form \(ax^2 + bx + c = 0\) (if any exist) can be found with the quadratic formula.
    \begin{equation*} x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    The value under the square root, \(b^2 - 4ac\text{,}\) is called the discriminant. The discriminant determines whether there will be two, one, or zero roots. (Note that we are only considering real-valued solutions.)
    🔗
    • If the discriminant is positive, there will be exactly two roots.
      🔗
      🔗
    • If the discriminant is zero, there will be exactly one root.
      🔗
      🔗
    • If the discriminant is negative, there will be no roots.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.1.4. Using the Quadratic Formula.

    Solve the following for \(x\text{.}\)
    1. \begin{equation*} 2x^2 - 0.3x - 5.1 = 0 \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    2. \begin{equation*} 0.5x^2 + x + 0.5 = 0 \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    3. \begin{equation*} -x^2 + 1.5x - 7 = 0 \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    4. \begin{equation*} 2x^2 - 3.2x + 1 = 10-0.4x \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    Answer.
    1. \(-1.524\) and \(1.674\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. \(\displaystyle -1\)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. No real solutions
      🔗
      🔗
    4. \(-1.534\) and \(2.934\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution.
    1.
    \begin{align*} x \amp= \frac{0.3 \pm \sqrt{(-0.3)^2 - 4(2)(-5.1)}}{2\cdot 2}\\ \amp= \frac{0.3 \pm \sqrt{40.89}}{4}\\ \amp\approx -1.524,\ 1.674. \end{align*}
    🔗
    2.
    \begin{align*} x \amp= \frac{-1 \pm \sqrt{(1)^2 - 4(0.5)(0.5)}}{2\cdot 0.5}\\ \amp= \frac{-1 \pm 0}{1}\\ \amp= -1. \end{align*}
    🔗
    3.
    \begin{align*} x \amp= \frac{-1.5 \pm \sqrt{(1.5)^2 - 4(-1)(-7)}}{2\cdot (-1)}\\ \amp= \frac{-1.5 \pm \sqrt{-25.75}}{-2} \end{align*}
    Since the discriminant is negative, there are no real solutions.
    🔗
    4. First, put the equation into the form \(ax^2 + bx + c = 0\text{.}\) We get \({2x^2 - 2.8x + 1 - 9 = 0}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    \begin{align*} x \amp= \frac{2.8 \pm \sqrt{(-2.8)^2 - 4(2)(-9)}}{2(2)}\\ \amp= \frac{2.8 \pm \sqrt{79.84}}{4}\\ \amp\approx -1.534,\ 2.934. \end{align*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 1.1.3 Solving "Hidden" Quadratic Equations

    Consider the following super-transcendental equation:
    \begin{equation*} e^{-x} + 3e^{-2x} = 5. \end{equation*}
    It’s ugly, and no one likes it. But it is solvable ``by hand.’’ This equation is actually a ``hidden’’ quadratic. To see this, make the substitution: \(z=e^{-x}\text{.}\) The equation then becomes \(z + 3z^2 = 5\) or equivalently \(3z^2 + z - 5 = 0\text{.}\) We can solve this using the quadratic formula:
    \begin{equation*} z = \frac{-1 \pm \sqrt{(1)^2 - 4(3)(-5)}}{2(3)} = -1.4684, 1.1350. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Those values are not the solutions, however. Recall that \(z=e^{-x}\text{.}\) So, what we have found is that \(e^{-x}=-1.4684\) or \(e^{-x}=1.1350\text{.}\) Since \(e^{-x}>0\text{,}\) we get \(e^{-x} = 1.1350\) or \(x = -0.1267\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Example 1.1.5. Solving a Hidden Quadratic.

    Solve the following equation for \(x\) with \(0\leq x \leq \pi\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} 2\sin^2(x+1) - 3\sin(x+1) = 3. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Answer.
    \(x=2.8988\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution.
    We make the substitution \(z = \sin(x+1)\) and put the equation in the proper form. We get \(2z^2 - 3z - 3 = 0\text{.}\) Using the quadratic formula, we get \(z = -0.686, 2.186.\) Since \(-1\leq \sin(x+1) \leq 1\text{,}\) we solve \({\sin(x+1) = -0.686}\text{.}\) Thus, \(x+1 = \sin^{-1}(-0.868) = 3.898\text{.}\) So, \({x = 2.898}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    Note that in the previous example, using a calculator to get \(\sin^{-1}(-0.686)\) will likely yield \(-0.75617\text{.}\) To get the proper \(x\text{,}\) value we use the fact that \(\sin(-0.75617) = \sin(\pi + 0.75617) = \sin(3.898)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Checkpoint 1.1.7. Solving a radical equation.

    Solve the following equation. Enter your answer to at least three decimal places.
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} 2x - \sqrt{14 - x^2} = 2 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Solution:
    🔗
    Answer.
    \(2.42481\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution.
    First, isolate the square root term:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} 2x - \sqrt{14 - x^2} \amp = 2 \\ -\sqrt{14 - x^2} \amp = 2 - 2x \\ \sqrt{14 - x^2} \amp = 2x - 2 \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Now square both sides:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} 14 - x^2 \amp = (2x - 2)^2 \\ 14 - x^2 \amp = (2^2)x^2 - 2(2)(2)x + 2^2 \\ 5x^2 - 8x - 10 \amp = 0 \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Solving the quadratic gives possible roots. \(-0.825\) and \(2.425\text{.}\) The smaller root turns out not to solve the original equation. So, we have one solution. \(2.425\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Checkpoint 1.1.8. Solving a hidden quadratic equation.

    Solve the quadratic equation
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} 4e^{5x} - 2e^{10x} = 1\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Smaller solution:
    🔗
    Larger solution:
    🔗
    Answer 1.
    \(-0.24559\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Answer 2.
    \(0.10696\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution.
    We can make a substitution \(t = e^{5x}\text{.}\) Then, we have \({4t - 2t^2 = 1}\text{.}\) We can re-write this as \({2t^2 - 4t + 1 = 0.}\)
    🔗
    We can use the quadratic formula:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} t = \frac{4 \pm \sqrt{16 - 8}}{4} \approx 0.29289, 1.70711. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Recall that \(t=e^{5x}\text{.}\) So, \(e^{5x} \approx 0.29289\) or \(e^{5x} \approx 1.70711.\) Solving gives \(x \approx -0.24559\) or \(x \approx 0.10696.\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Checkpoint 1.1.9. Solving a hidden quadratic equation again.

    Solve the quadratic equation
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} 8(x+1)^2 - 3(x+1)^4 + 5 = 0\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Give your answer to at least three decimal places.
    🔗
    Smaller solution:
    🔗
    Larger solution:
    🔗
    Answer 1.
    \(-2.78585\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Answer 2.
    \(0.78585\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution.
    We can make a substitution \(z = (x+1)^2\text{.}\) Then, we have \({8z - 3z^2 +5 = 0}\text{.}\) We can re-write this as \({3z^2 - 8z - 5 = 0.}\)
    🔗
    We can use the quadratic formula:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} z = \frac{8 \pm \sqrt{64 + 60}}{6} \approx -0.52259, 3.18926. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Recall that \(z=(x+1)^2\text{.}\) So, \((x+1)^2 = -0.52259\) or \((x+1)^2 = 3.18926.\) This first gives non-real solutions. Solving \((x+1)^2 = 3.18926\) gives \(x \approx -2.78585\) or \(x \approx 0.78585.\)

    Checkpoint 1.1.10. Solving a hidden quadratic equation yet again.

    The moment generating function for a discrete random variable is given by \(M_X(t) = 0.7e^{3t} + 0.3e^{6t}.\) For which value is \(M_X(t)\) equal to 3.9? Give your answer to at least three decimal places.
    🔗
    Solution:
    🔗
    Answer.
    \(0.32143\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution.
    We can make a substitution \(z = e^{3t}\text{.}\) Then, we have \({0.7z + 0.3z^2 = 3.9}\text{.}\) We can re-write this as \({0.3z^2 + 0.7z - 3.9 = 0.}\)
    🔗
    We can use the quadratic formula:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} z = \frac{-0.7 \pm \sqrt{0.49 + 4.68}}{0.6} \approx -4.95627, 2.62294. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Recall that \(z=e^{3t}\text{.}\) So, \(e^{3t} = -4.95627\) or \(e^{3t} = 2.62294.\) The first equation has no solution. Solving the second equation gives \(t \approx 0.32143\text{.}\)
